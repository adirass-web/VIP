The Threat is Already in the Family

In inheritance disputes, access was never taken — it was given, usually with the best intentions. A sibling who helped with tech. A caregiver who managed the phone. A family member who "just needed the password." The access was legitimate. The moment it becomes a weapon is when the relationship changes.

Apple ID / iCloud

The sibling who set up the iPad

When their father got an iPad three years ago, his older son had set it up — entered the Apple ID, configured the settings, and stayed signed in "in case there's a problem." After the father's death, the older son still had access to the iCloud account: photos, messages, health data, and the Notes app where the father had kept a running list of changes he wanted to make to his will. His younger siblings did not know this access existed.

The caregiver who answered the messages

For the last year of her life, an elderly woman's caregiver had "helped" with her WhatsApp — reading incoming messages, drafting responses, deciding what to pass on and what to ignore. The family members outside the home believed they were communicating directly with their mother. In the months before her death, while the will was being changed, they were not. Nobody had set out to deceive anyone. The access had simply been given, and never examined.
