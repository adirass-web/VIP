The Threat is Already Inside

In most small business partnerships, access is never formally managed. It is granted in trust, built up over years, and never revisited — until the moment someone leaves and you discover what "shared access" meant in practice.

WhatsApp Business Group

The group nobody cleaned

Three partners had run a business WhatsApp group for three years — vendors, logistics updates, pricing decisions. When one partner left, nobody removed him. Two months later, a new supplier negotiation started in the same group. He was still in it, reading every message, watching the margin discussions unfold in real time. By the time either of the remaining partners noticed, he had already approached the same supplier.

Google Drive

The folder link that never expired

They had shared a Google Drive folder at the start of the partnership — "for the project." The link was still active three years later. After the split, the departing partner could see every new file added to it: updated pricing sheets, new client proposals, a draft of the legal strategy their attorney had uploaded for review. Nobody had thought to revoke a link that had always just worked.

Personal Gmail as Business Email

The account that ran the business

The company had started with one partner's personal Gmail. Both partners had the password — it was practical at the time. After the dispute started, the departing partner still had full access to four years of business correspondence: every client email, every vendor negotiation, every conversation with the company attorney about the split itself. The password had never been changed because there had never been a reason to.
