The Threat is Closer Than You Think

None of the cases below involved a hack. No passwords were cracked. No professionals were hired. In each one, the access was already there — built into the relationship, never designed to end when the relationship did.

WhatsApp Web

The session nobody logged out

He moved out in March. The desktop computer stayed. His WhatsApp Web session was still active on it — every message he sent and received visible on the screen in the living room, including a running thread with his attorney about asset division strategy and which accounts he planned to transfer before proceedings formalised. She took screenshots for four weeks before he realised the session was open.

Instagram → Gmail → Google Drive

The reused password

She had used the same password since 2017 — her Instagram, her Gmail, her Google account. After the separation, he tried her Instagram password on her Gmail. It worked. In Google Drive, he found the folder she had been building with her attorney for three months: property valuations, undisclosed bank statements, a detailed asset summary her legal strategy depended on keeping private.

PlayStation Family Account → OneDrive

The gaming account that connected everything

They had set up a PlayStation family account so the kids could play online. It was linked to his Microsoft account, which was part of a Microsoft family group. His OneDrive — where he had been quietly moving financial documents and records of an account she didn't know existed — was accessible through the family group. She found it while looking for a school project the children had saved.

Smart Doorbell

The camera that stayed on his account

He had installed a smart doorbell at the family home three years earlier. After he moved out, the camera stayed. So did his account — still sending motion alerts, still streaming to his phone. For five months, he could see who came and went: when her attorney visited, when her sister arrived with documents, when someone he didn't recognise stayed past midnight. She assumed it was a security device. She didn't know whose security it was serving.
