## Part 1 — The "Real Risk" Section

### The Core Argument

The conventional threat model — hacker in a hoodie, sophisticated attack, state-level adversary — is not what threatens the people who come to this site. Their threat is someone they know. Someone who already has their passwords. Someone who remembers the birthday they used as their PIN. Someone who is in their family photo album.

The argument has two parts:

**Part A — The threat is personal, not technical.**
The people trying to access your devices and accounts are not criminals who have never met you. They are your spouse, your business partner, your sibling, your former co-founder. They know your habits. They may already have access — through shared accounts, family plans, shared credentials. The breach doesn't start with a cyberattack. It starts with a relationship.

**Part B — Your defenses were built for strangers, not for people who know you.**
Most security advice is designed for external, anonymous threats: use a strong password, don't click suspicious links, install antivirus. None of it addresses the person who knows your strong password because you gave it to them, who clicks the same links you do because they're on your account, and whose device was the same trusted device all along.

This is the gap. And it is precisely the gap this service closes.

#### Evidence Block 1 — Divorce & Separation

**Subheading:** When your partner becomes your opponent

> The data does not require speculation. In a 2024 survey, 85% of people in committed relationships had given their partner access to personal accounts — and 45% reported that after a breakup, that access was used against them. Digital evidence now appears in more than 80% of contested divorce cases. The most common sources: WhatsApp conversations, iCloud photo libraries, shared Google accounts, location history, and banking apps that never had their login changed.
>
> This is not surveillance. This is access that was granted during the relationship and never revoked. The threat arrives not as a hack, but as a login — with the correct password, from a device that was once trusted.

*Sources: Malwarebytes "What's Mine is Yours" survey, 2024; American Academy of Matrimonial Lawyers, cited 2024.*

---

#### Evidence Block 2 — Business Dispute

**Subheading:** Your former partner still has the keys

> When a business relationship turns adversarial, the digital exposure is immediate and structural. In a typical small business, passwords are shared, cloud accounts are jointly held, and there has never been a reason to separate what is "yours" from what is "ours." A third of employees who leave an organisation retain active access to company systems after their departure — not because anyone broke in, but because no one revoked it.
>
> Trade secret theft in business disputes is, in the vast majority of cases, not external hacking. It is someone with existing access using it — emailing files to a personal account, syncing documents to a private Dropbox, exporting client lists before the dispute becomes legal. The most common methods: email, a USB drive, a shared cloud folder. The most common window: the 30 days immediately after the relationship breaks down.

*Sources: IS Decisions / CERT Insider Threat Database (one in three ex-employees retains access); Verizon DBIR 2025; Carnegie Mellon CERT, widely cited on 70% / 30-day window; CMU/SEI-2011-TN-006 on exfiltration methods.*

---

#### Evidence Block 3 — Inheritance Clash

**Subheading:** Family trust is a digital liability

> In inheritance disputes, the threat is almost always internal. Studies consistently show that family members account for the majority of elder financial abuse — not strangers. And the mechanism is rarely sophisticated. It is a sibling who was given their parents' banking login "just in case." A family member who knows the email password because they helped set it up. An adult child who has been managing a parent's accounts for years and sees no reason to stop.
>
> Courts increasingly have to rule on what should be routine questions: who can access the deceased's email, who can view the cloud-stored estate documents, who has authority over the accounts. These disputes arise because access was never governed — it was assumed. And once a dispute begins, uncontrolled access is a liability, not a convenience.

*Sources: NCOA / Nursing Home Abuse Center (family members account for 47–62% of elder financial abuse); FinCEN Financial Trend Analysis 2023; Matter of Serrano, 2017 (NY Surrogate's Court — courts litigating routine digital account access in estates).*

---

#### Evidence Block 4 — Commercial Spying

**Subheading:** The leak came from inside the company

> The most expensive assumption a founder or executive can make is that their security threat is external. Research consistently shows that 85% of trade secret theft is committed by someone the company knows — an employee, a contractor, a departing co-founder. The most common method is not a cyberattack. It is an engineer emailing source code to a personal account, or syncing proprietary documents to a private cloud folder before their last day.
>
> Nearly all senior executives use personal devices for sensitive business communications — and most have no security controls on those devices whatsoever. The M&A thread that leaked, the product roadmap that ended up with a competitor, the client list that followed an employee to a rival firm — in almost every case, the access point was personal. A personal phone. A personal email. A personal cloud account that no policy ever reached.

*Sources: Fair Competition Law analysis of federal court cases (85% insider rate); CMU CERT / DTEX 2026 (59–70% of departing employees take confidential data; 70% within 30 days of resignation); Agency "Not Cyber Safe for Work" report 2023 (97% of executives use personal devices for work; 87% have no security tools on personal devices).*

### Implementation Notes for the Real Risk Section

**Tone:** Calm, factual, not alarmist. The evidence is alarming enough — the copy should present it soberly. No exclamation points. No "shocking statistics." Let the numbers do the work.

**Attribution style:** Cite the source at the end of each block in italics (as drafted above). Do not use footnote numbers — they feel academic and slow the read. A simple source note at paragraph end is enough for credibility without interrupting flow.

**What to avoid:** Do not frame this as "most people don't know X." Condescension kills trust. Present the reality, let the reader recognise themselves in it.

**Length:** On the homepage, use the opening paragraph + one of the four evidence blocks (the most relevant to the average visitor — divorce is likely the highest-volume vertical). The dedicated page carries all four. Each vertical page can carry its own block inline.

**Visual treatment:** Consider a simple 2-column layout on desktop: statistic callout on the left (large type, sourced), explanatory paragraph on the right. On mobile, stacked. No infographics needed — the numbers speak clearly in plain text.

## Part 2 — Full Content Strategy

### The Current State

The site is six pages: index, four vertical landing pages, founder. Every page is a conversion landing page optimised for warm referral traffic. There is no content that earns trust from cold visitors, no surface area for Google to index for informational queries, and no reason for attorneys or accountants to share it with colleagues who haven't already heard of the service.

The business model is referral-driven. Content does not replace the referral channel. It supports three specific functions:

1. **Bridging cold visitors** who arrive from search, LinkedIn, or someone forwarding a link — giving them somewhere to land before they're ready to contact
2. **Arming referral partners** — giving attorneys, accountants, and mediators something credible to share with clients and colleagues
3. **Building Dr. Tabansky's authority** in professional communities (LinkedIn, press, speaking) in a way that generates more referrals

Every content piece below maps to at least one of these three functions.

### Priority 2 — "What Your Attorney Can't Protect You From" (referral partner magnet)

**What it is:** A short, professional-register article (800–1,200 words) written from the perspective of what family law attorneys, estate attorneys, and business litigators observe about the digital dimension of disputes — and why it falls outside the scope of legal representation.

**Why it works:** Attorneys share content that makes them look knowledgeable and caring to their clients. An article that helps an attorney explain digital risk to a divorce client — without the attorney having to become a cybersecurity expert — is directly useful to them. It also positions Dr. Tabansky as a colleague, not a vendor.

**Angle:** "You have excellent legal counsel. Your attorney is protecting you in court, in mediation, and in negotiation. But there is a dimension of your situation that legal representation does not reach — and that dimension is open right now."

**Structure:**
- What attorneys see: how digital evidence shows up in family and commercial litigation (cite the 80% of contested divorces statistic)
- What attorneys cannot do: they cannot harden your devices, revoke your ex-partner's access to shared accounts, or stop your data from being accessed before proceedings begin
- What the digital dimension actually looks like: concrete examples by vertical
- What the right sequence is: protect first, then proceed

**Placement:** Dedicated page, linked from homepage and founder page. Also exists as a PDF download — this is the document a family law attorney can print and put in a client folder. The PDF version carries Dr. Tabansky's contact details and the "call this number" card concept.

**CTA at end:** "Share with your clients" button (copies the URL) + WhatsApp CTA for clients who are reading it directly.

### Priority 3 — Anonymised Case Vignettes (conversion support)

**What they are:** Four short (150–200 word) third-person narratives — one per vertical — that describe a realistic situation and outcome. Not testimonials (which would compromise client confidentiality). Not fabricated case studies. Constructed composite situations that are accurate to the type of engagement.

**Why they work:** Abstract risk does not convert. Concrete situations do. "A technology executive in Tel Aviv was in the middle of an acquisition negotiation when a competitor appeared at a client meeting with information they should not have had" is more effective than any statistics paragraph. It triggers recognition.

**Format on site:** Each vignette lives on its relevant vertical page, between the threat description and the pricing table. Short enough to read in 30 seconds. Structured as: situation → what happened → what was done → outcome.

**Critical note:** Frame as "a situation like this" not "a client told us." Never claim these are actual client accounts. The confidentiality positioning is the whole product — real client stories, even anonymised, would undermine the "no logs, no trace" promise.

**Sample vignette — Commercial Spying:**
> A founding team learned that a competitor had bid on one of their key accounts using pricing and project details they had never disclosed publicly. The leak was eventually traced to messaging threads between the founders and their sales lead — conversations that had been happening on personal phones, with no separation from personal email accounts that had been compromised months earlier. By the time the source was identified, two more accounts had already received competitive bids. The full device and account hardening took one day. The damage assessment took considerably longer.

### Priority 4 — FAQ Expansion (cold visitor bridge + SEO)

**The current FAQ** (on the homepage) handles warm-visitor objections: Can you hack my partner back? Is this like antivirus? Do you copy my data? These are the right questions for someone who has already decided they need this kind of service.

**Cold visitors ask different questions:**
- "How do I know if my spouse has access to my phone?"
- "Can my ex see my WhatsApp messages?"
- "How do I know if someone has installed spyware on my phone?"
- "What digital evidence can be used in a divorce in Israel?"
- "How do I protect my business communications during a legal dispute?"
- "Can a business partner access my Google Drive after we split?"

These are search queries. They also happen to be the exact questions people type into Google at 2am before they've called their attorney. A dedicated FAQ page — or an expanded FAQ section with these questions — gives the site search surface area for crisis-moment queries and gives cold visitors a reason to stay.

**Format:** Separate `/en/faq.html` page. 10–15 questions, organised by situation cluster. Each answer is 2–4 sentences — enough to be useful, short enough to not exhaust the reader before they contact. Each answer ends with a single-sentence bridge to the relevant vertical page or the Threat Assessment CTA.

**Hebrew/Russian equivalents essential:** The highest-volume search queries in this market will be in Hebrew and Russian. The FAQ must be translated at the same priority level as the main pages.

**Additions that increase its utility:**

**A short video (optional but high-value):** 60–90 seconds, Dr. Tabansky speaking directly to camera, explaining the service in plain language. Not a production — a single, well-lit take. Video on the founder page dramatically increases time-on-site and conversion rate for cold visitors. The Washington Post and AP credibility markers are visible in still images; a video makes the person real.

### Priority 6 — LinkedIn Articles (referral network authority)

**What they are:** 4–6 short LinkedIn articles (600–900 words each) published by Dr. Tabansky, targeting attorneys, accountants, and business professionals. Written in the collegial voice of an expert who has seen specific things, not a vendor promoting a service.

**Why LinkedIn specifically:** The referral network — family attorneys, forensic accountants, mediators, estate lawyers, business litigators — lives on LinkedIn. A published article that gets shared by one attorney reaches dozens of their colleagues. It makes Dr. Tabansky's name recognisable before the referral is ever made.

**Proposed article topics:**

1. **"What I Find on Every Device During a Divorce — And What It Means for Attorneys"**
   Practical, specific, collegial. Describes common access vulnerabilities that show up in divorce-adjacent device hardening engagements. Ends with what attorneys can tell clients to do before filing.

2. **"The Business Dispute Nobody Sees Coming: Shared Cloud Access After a Partner Leaves"**
   For business litigators and forensic accountants. Describes how departing partners retain cloud and account access, and what happens when it's not revoked before the dispute becomes formal.

3. **"Why 'Just Change Your Password' Is Not Enough — And What Is"**
   Educational, broadly applicable. Explains why generic security advice fails in high-stakes personal situations. Readable by a professional who will share it with clients.

4. **"Three Things That Happen to Your Digital Life When a Family Dispute Goes Legal"**
   Inheritance/estate focus. Written for estate attorneys and financial advisors who work with family conflict.

5. **"The Personal Device Problem: Why Senior Executives Are the Easiest Targets in a Dispute"**
   For corporate attorneys and board advisors. Anchored in the 97% of executives use personal devices for sensitive communications statistic.

Each article ends with: "Dr. Tabansky works with individuals in high-stakes disputes, in person, in Israel. For professional enquiries: [LinkedIn message / WhatsApp]."

**Publish cadence:** One article per month. Share to relevant attorney and accountant LinkedIn groups. Each article can also be repurposed as a page on the site for SEO value.
