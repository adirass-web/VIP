### Narrative paragraph — Divorce/Separation

> The data does not require speculation. In a 2024 survey, 85% of people in committed relationships had given their partner access to personal accounts — and 45% reported that after a breakup, that access was abused for stalking or surveillance. Digital evidence now appears in more than 80% of contested divorce cases. The most common sources: WhatsApp conversations, iCloud photo libraries, shared Google accounts, location history from a family plan that was never cancelled.
>
> In Israel, a 2016 ruling by the Netanya Rabbinical Tribunal established that content secretly retrieved from a spouse's phone is admissible evidence in divorce proceedings. A Haaretz investigation documented that spyware developed for counterterrorism has been used by separating couples for years. In the UK, Refuge reports a 258% rise in technology-facilitated abuse between 2018 and 2022 — and 72% of survivors experienced it.
>
> This is not surveillance by sophisticated adversaries. It is access that was granted inside the relationship and never revoked. The breach arrives not as a hack but as a login — with the correct password, from a device that was once trusted.

*Sources: Malwarebytes "What's Mine is Yours" survey 2024; Malwarebytes October 2023; American Academy of Matrimonial Lawyers 2024; Avast/Gen Digital June 2023; Refuge UK 2019–2025; Haaretz July 2019; law.co.il June 2016.*

### Framing paragraph — Personal IT vignettes

> The statistics describe scale. The cases below describe mechanism — the specific, ordinary ways that access built into a shared life becomes a liability when the relationship ends.
>
> None of these required technical skill. None involved a breach in the conventional sense. Each one began with something that felt reasonable at the time: a shared subscription, a family plan, a session on a shared computer, a password that was the same everywhere because it had always been enough.
>
> The exposure was not created by the separation. It was created by the relationship. The separation simply changed whose interests that access served.

### Personal IT Vignettes — How It Actually Happens

*Based on true cases, details obfuscated to protect the identities of those involved.*

---

**1. WhatsApp Web — The Session Nobody Logged Out**

> He moved out in March. The desktop computer stayed.
>
> His WhatsApp Web session was still active on it — the green dot, the conversation list, everything. He hadn't logged out. She hadn't known to look. For six weeks, every message he sent and received was visible on the screen in the living room: conversations with his brother, updates to a friend, and — the ones that mattered — a running thread with his attorney about asset division strategy, what to disclose and when, and which accounts he planned to transfer before the proceedings formalised.
>
> She took screenshots for four weeks before he realised the session was open.

---

**2. The Reused Password — Instagram to Gmail to Google Drive**

> She had used the same password since 2017. Her Instagram. Her Gmail. Her Google account. It had never seemed like a problem — who would think to try it?
>
> After the separation, he did. He tried her Instagram password on her Gmail. It worked. In Google Drive, he found the folder she had been building with her attorney for three months: property valuations, undisclosed bank statements, a detailed asset summary that her legal strategy depended on keeping private.
>
> She had never told him the password. She hadn't needed to. It was the same one she'd been using for everything since before they met.

---

**3. PlayStation Family Account → Microsoft → OneDrive**

> They had set up a PlayStation family account so the kids could play online. It was connected to his Microsoft account, which was part of a Microsoft family group. He had never thought about what that meant for storage.
>
> His OneDrive — where he had been quietly moving financial documents, contracts, and records of a secondary account she didn't know existed — was accessible through the family group. She found it while looking for a school project the children had saved.
>
> He had been moving documents there for eight months, believing it was private. The family plan he'd set up for the children had connected everything.

---

**4. iCloud Location Sharing — Neither of Them Turned It Off**

> They had turned on location sharing two years earlier when he travelled for work. It seemed practical. Neither of them thought about it after he moved out.
>
> For four months, she could see his location in real time — his new apartment, which clinic he visited on which days, which attorney's office he went to and how long he stayed, and an address in the north of the city he visited most Friday evenings.
>
> She never told him she knew. She mentioned none of it to her attorney until it had accumulated into something useful.

---

**5. The Shared Google Photos Album**

> The family album had been running for six years — holidays, children's birthdays, a decade of ordinary life. After the separation, the album stayed open. Google Photos kept syncing from everyone's phones.
>
> He moved into a new apartment. He started a new life. The photos he took in his new home — including ones that were not meant for anyone else — were syncing to the same shared album his wife still had access to. He didn't remember that the album existed. He hadn't opened it in two years.

---

**6. The Security Camera That Stayed on His Account**

> He had installed a smart doorbell at the family home three years earlier. After he moved out, the camera stayed. So did his account. The device was still registered to his login — still sending motion alerts, still recording, still streaming to his phone.
>
> For five months, he could see who came and went. When her attorney visited. When her sister arrived with documents. When someone he didn't recognise stayed past midnight. He watched from his phone, in his new apartment, saying nothing.
>
> She assumed the doorbell was a security device. She didn't know whose security it was serving.

---

**7. Telegram Desktop — The Session on the Laptop He Kept**

> She used Telegram to coordinate with her financial advisor and her legal team. The desktop app was installed on a laptop that had stayed at the house — she had switched to her work computer and forgotten about it.
>
> He had kept the laptop. He didn't need a password. The session was already open. The message history was already there.

### Note on חוקר פרטי (Private Investigator) — Hebrew page only

In Israel, hiring a private investigator during contested divorce proceedings is common and widely known. Attorneys on both sides routinely anticipate it. The חוקר פרטי's toolkit is primarily digital: reconstructing location history from shared apps and family plans, accessing communications through shared cloud accounts, documenting financial activity through joint banking logins. They do not create the exposure — they exploit the one that already exists.

**Recommended addition to the Hebrew divorce page (one sentence, in the evidence section):**

> *In Israel, it is common for one party in a contested divorce to engage a private investigator — חוקר פרטי — during proceedings. Their methods are primarily digital: location data from shared apps, access through accounts that were never formally separated, cloud backups that remained connected after the relationship ended. They do not need to break in. They find the door that was left open.*

Framing note: the threat is the spouse. The חוקר פרטי is the instrument the spouse may hire to exploit the client's existing digital exposure. Keep this framing — do not present the investigator as the adversary.

## Commercial Spying — Revised Evidence Block (Israeli Examples)

These replace the earlier high-end / US-centric cases. The persona is a small or medium Israeli business owner — a contractor, a studio, a professional services firm — not a Fortune 500 executive.

---

**Direction 1 — The Air Conditioning Contractor Who Took the Service Contracts**
*(Based on a true case, details obfuscated to protect the identities of those involved)*

> Two partners ran an HVAC installation and maintenance company in the Sharon region. One handled operations, the other managed client accounts and the company's CRM — contact information, maintenance schedules, service contract terms, pricing history, and which clients were due for renewal and when.
>
> When the partnership ended, the departing partner had 400 active client records in a system he had always accessed freely. He opened a competing operation within three months. The first calls his new business made were to clients whose contracts were expiring in the next 60 days — the same list he had exported the week before he left.
>
> The original company lost approximately 40% of its renewal pipeline before understanding where the calls were coming from. By then, the access had not been revoked for four months.

---

**Direction 2 — The Yoga Studio That Discovered Its Instructor Had Been Managing Its Business**
*(Based on a true case, details obfuscated to protect the identities of those involved)*

> A Tel Aviv boutique fitness studio gave its senior instructor access to the booking system — client accounts, package pricing, and payment records — to help manage scheduling. The arrangement had existed for two years.
>
> When the instructor left to open her own studio, the client data left with her. She had the email addresses, the mobile numbers, the membership history, and — because the payment system stored it — the price each client had agreed to pay. Her opening promotional offer was priced precisely below the packages most likely to convince current clients to switch.
>
> The studio owner did not know the instructor had been managing more than the schedule until clients started cancelling.

---

**Direction 3 — The Investor Who Sat on the Board Until He Backed the Competitor**
*(Documented pattern — specific details composited from multiple sources)*

> A seed investor in an Israeli B2B SaaS company held a board seat for three years. During that time, he received quarterly board packs: revenue data, gross margin by product line, customer churn, pipeline by stage, hiring plans, and product roadmaps. He was in the company's Slack channels. He had been granted read access to the shared data room that was never formally closed.
>
> Eighteen months after he reduced his position, he led a seed round in a competing company in the same space. The founders of the original company discovered this through LinkedIn. What they could not determine — and still cannot — is precisely what informed his decision, his timing, and the competitive strategy advice he gave to the new portfolio company.
>
> Legal "ethical walls" between a fund's portfolio companies are not enforceable in a seed-stage environment. The information was shared in trust. The conflict of interest arrived later.

*Sources: Wilson Sonsini Goodrich & Rosati, "Board Positions and Competing Portfolio Companies"; IOSCO Private Equity Conflicts of Interest Report; Fenwick & West on CVC conflicts.*

---

**Direction 4 — The Hair Salon Whose Staff Took the Client Database**
*(Real case — Pennsylvania, 2013)*

> A hair salon sued five stylists who left together — without notice — and joined a competitor three days later. Before leaving, they had accessed the salon's password-protected database: client names, appointment history, styling preferences, pricing. No hacking was required. They had the login. They had always had the login.
>
> This is the most common form of commercial theft in small and medium businesses. Not corporate espionage — people who worked alongside you, knew your systems, and used access that was never designed with a dispute in mind.

*Source: DFK Inc. v. former employees, Pennsylvania, 2013.*

---

### Narrative paragraph — Commercial Spying

> In small and medium businesses, competitive intelligence does not arrive from the outside. It walks out the door with someone who had the login.
>
> The pattern is consistent across sectors. An HVAC contractor exports the renewal pipeline on his last week. A fitness instructor has managed the booking system long enough to know which clients pay the most. A financial advisor emails the client list to a personal account before his termination is formalised. In each case, the access was legitimate, granted in good faith, and designed for cooperation — not for the possibility that the relationship would end in competition.
>
> The access that enables the business is the same access that enables the breach. Nobody changed the password because there had never been a reason to. By the time there was a reason, the information had already moved.

*Sources: Based on true cases, details obfuscated to protect identities where noted; DFK Inc. v. employees (Pennsylvania, 2013); IIA Israel (Hebrew, partner business access fraud); Wilson Sonsini / IOSCO on VC board conflicts; Foley & Lardner insider threat analysis (85% of trade secret cases involve insiders).*

## Inheritance — Revised Evidence Block with Israeli Sources

### Key Israeli source: The Digital Inheritance Law (2024)

Israel is, as of July 2024, the first country in the world with a law governing posthumous access to digital content. The law was prompted by the October 7 attacks — within months, more than 1,200 families petitioned the Guardian General (האפוטרופוס הכללי) for access to deceased victims' social media accounts. Many of these accounts contained final messages, images, and videos that families could not access because of platform privacy policies.

The law now compels digital platforms to publish clear posthumous access policies. But it also crystallises something that inheritance attorneys in Israel have known for years: digital accounts hold real value in an estate — communications, financial records, documents — and access to them is contested.

This context is useful for the site in two ways: it names the specific Israeli legal institution (Guardian General / רשם הירושות), and it establishes that digital access in inheritance contexts is a nationally recognised issue, not an abstract risk.

---

### The "Digital Isolation" pattern — Israeli legal sources

A 2026 article on the Israeli notary and inheritance law platform best-notary.co.il introduces the concept of **בידוד דיגיטלי** (digital isolation) as a form of undue influence — blocking a testator's WhatsApp access, deleting messages, manipulating social media feeds to increase dependency on a caregiver or family member. The article covers deepfake will fraud and password control as mechanisms of testamentary manipulation.

Israeli courts have recognised patterns of caregiver-driven isolation leading to will fraud:
- An 80-year-old psychiatrist, isolated during COVID, changed his will to leave 8 million shekels and an apartment to a young cafe owner he had met. Family members challenged in court on grounds of psychological manipulation during isolation (Kikar HaShabbat reporting).
- A professional caregiver systematically disconnected an elderly woman from her extended family, took financial control, and received property via an altered will. The court voided the relevant provisions after examining the isolation tactics and timing (Dr. Sharon Perling, Law Office, lawadvice.co.il).

---

### Narrative paragraph — Inheritance

> In inheritance disputes, the threat is almost always internal. Studies across multiple jurisdictions consistently show that family members account for the majority of elder financial abuse — not strangers, not criminals. The mechanism is rarely sophisticated. It is a sibling who was given the banking login "just in case." A family member who knows the email password because they helped set up the account. A caregiver who has been managing the parent's phone for months and sees no reason to stop.
>
> Israeli courts have documented a specific pattern: what legal practitioners now call digital isolation — the systematic blocking of a vulnerable person's communications, control of their accounts and devices, manipulation of who they can reach and what they can read. In documented cases, this has preceded altered wills, changed beneficiary designations, and contested estates that might have been uncontested.
>
> Israel is the first country in the world to legislate posthumous access to digital content — a law passed in 2024, prompted in part by the October 7 attacks, when over 1,200 families petitioned courts for access to deceased victims' accounts. The fact that legislation was needed confirms what estate attorneys here have known for years: digital accounts are estate assets, access to them is contested, and the absence of formal governance is a vulnerability.

*Sources: Israeli Posthumous Access to Digital Content Act (5784-2024), July 2024; Pearl Cohen PCDK; best-notary.co.il 2026 (Hebrew); Kikar HaShabbat (8M shekel COVID isolation case, Hebrew); Dr. Sharon Perling / lawadvice.co.il (Hebrew); NCOA / Nursing Home Abuse Center (family members 47–62% of elder financial abuse); FinCEN Financial Trend Analysis 2023.*

## Business Dispute — Revised Evidence Block (Fictional Israeli Example)

### The scenario the site needs to reach

The persona is not a Fortune 500 executive. It is two people who built something together — a small grocery chain, an accounting practice, a logistics company, a car dealership — and one of them is leaving, or has left, and the other is about to discover what "shared access" meant in practice.

---

**The Third Partner Who Opened a Store Next Door**
*(Based on a true case, details obfuscated to protect the identities of those involved)*

> Three partners ran a small grocery chain with four locations in the central region. For eight years, all three had full access to the shared accounting software — and a cloud folder where monthly P&L statements had been kept since the business started. Nobody had ever thought to restrict access. The system was built for a business where everyone trusted everyone.
>
> When the third partner sold his stake, the exit was legally clean. What it was not was digitally clean. In the weeks before the formal transfer, he had downloaded five years of financial records — not because anyone had told him he couldn't, but because he had always been able to.
>
> Three months later, he opened a competing grocery operation two streets away. The conflict that followed was not simply about proximity. It was about what he knew. The financial records he had copied documented a number of practices — in purchasing, in supplier payments, in cash handling — that none of the three partners wanted examined by a tax authority. He knew this. They knew he knew this. The negotiation that followed was not a business dispute. It was leverage.
>
> The attorneys on both sides spent eight months on a conflict that, at its core, was about a cloud folder nobody had thought to close.

---

### The structural weakness: no exit process for access

> In most small business partnerships, access is managed on trust, not policy. One accounting software login shared by all partners. A cloud folder with no expiry date on sharing permissions. A WhatsApp group that includes the person who left last month. Nobody revoked anything because there had never been a documented reason to.
>
> When the business relationship turns adversarial, that access does not disappear. It is still there — and the person who has it may know things they are not supposed to know. Financial records that were copied before the split. Accounting data that documents irregularities neither party wants disclosed. Client lists that can be taken to a new business opened three streets away.
>
> This is not hypothetical. It is the standard pattern in partnership disputes involving small and medium businesses: unrestricted mutual access during the relationship, no formal exit process when it ends, and a dispute shaped significantly by what one party was able to copy before the other thought to check.

*Sources: Based on a true case, details obfuscated to protect the identities of those involved; ACFE 2024 Report to the Nations (32% of fraud cases involve lack of internal controls; SMBs with <100 employees face median loss of $141,000 due to fewer controls); Cybiz, Israeli cybersecurity firm (64% of Israeli SMBs assessed as vulnerable); IIA Israel (Hebrew, construction partnership case); CISA 2024 on shared credential failure in SMBs.*
